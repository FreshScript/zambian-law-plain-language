Employment and Workers’ Rights in Zambia

Everyone who works in Zambia has rights protected by law. Whether you work in an office, factory, farm, or as a casual worker, knowing your rights helps you stay safe and be treated fairly.

Employment Contracts

Most jobs require a contract — this is a written or spoken agreement between you and your employer. It should explain:

  • Your job duties
  • Working hours and days
  • How much you will be paid and when
  • Leave (holidays, sick leave, maternity/paternity leave)
  • How to end the job (notice period)

Working Hours and Overtime

  • The normal working week is 8 hours a day, 48 hours per week.
  • Overtime must be paid extra, usually at 1.5 times the normal wage.
  • Workers have the right to rest breaks and at least one day off each week.

Wages and Payment

  • You must be paid at least the minimum wage, set by the government.
  • Your employer must pay you on time and not hold your salary without good reason.
  • Deductions from salary are only allowed if you agree or it is required by law (e.g., taxes, social security).

Leave Rights

  • Annual leave: At least 21 days paid holiday per year.
  • Sick leave: Paid leave if you are ill, supported by a medical certificate.
  • Maternity leave: 84 days paid leave for women, with protection against dismissal.
  • Paternity leave: Some employers offer it, though not always required by law.

Unfair Dismissal and Termination

Your employer cannot fire you without a valid reason. Reasons must be fair and follow legal procedure:

  • Warning and chance to improve if you perform poorly.
  • Following company rules and contracts.
  • Proper notice period before ending the contract.

Workplace Safety and Health

Employers must provide a safe working environment and protect workers from accidents or illnesses.

  • Use protective equipment if needed.
  • Report hazards or unsafe conditions.
  • Access to first aid and emergency help.

Workers’ Rights to Organize

You have the right to join or form a trade union to protect your interests and negotiate better conditions.

Where to Get Help

  • Labour Office: Offers mediation and advice on disputes.
  • Trade Unions: Can support members with legal advice and representation.
  • Legal Aid Clinics: Help workers who cannot afford lawyers.

Knowing your rights helps you work with dignity and fairness. Don’t be afraid to ask questions or seek help.
